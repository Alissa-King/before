Still Burning

VI

0:00

Every star you ever see could be gone
before it's seen —
already past tense, and you can't yet sense
the lag, the slip,
the sleight of the distance
between the looking and the light,
the glance and the thing —
you stand there,
call it now,
like a glance is the same as a grip,
like the fire that finds you means the fire behind you still burns.

But that's the trick of it.

The mercy and the lie.

The way the dead keep visiting the eye.

And maybe memory's like this:
not a room you can revisit,
but a signal still in transit —
a fire that already finished,
still arriving, undiminished, in the chest, like proof.

I am full of light from extinguished things.

The Tuesday you turned seven in the rain,
still wet, still plain
as the hour it fell.

The kitchen the morning everything broke —
the coffee going cold,
the ordinary hum of a day too young to know
the weight of what it would become —

still happening.

Every one of them still happening,
the wave still cresting in the swell of the now,
the way the light won't mourn its source,
won't look back, won't grieve the course it's on,
won't grasp that there's nothing left behind it —

it just goes.

It just keeps on, and blind, and arriving.

That's the thing physics can't explain:
the past is not behind you.
It's incoming.
It left when it left and it's still en route,
still coming, still humming its way toward here.

So when I say I am seven,
I am thirty,
I am the woman in the lot who finally caught
what she'd been surviving —
I mean they're all still arriving.

I mean not one of them has landed yet.

I mean I'm the sky that can't forget,
that holds a hundred dying suns
and calls the whole of it beautiful,
because I cannot tell which ones are gone,
which still burn, which only seem to,
which left a light I'm leaning on.

And I have pressed my face to the glass
and watched the old selves pass
in a hundred lit windows going by —
the girl with her knees drawn high,
the one who said yes when the truth was no,
the one who learned to mean it,
the one who still can't know
if she learned a thing or just went quiet in a lower key —
and not one of them turns to see that I'm watching.
Not one of them feels the light they're still throwing.

They just burned.

They burned the only way they learned:
forward, and outward, and gone
into whatever came.

Astronomers call it lookback time —
the deeper the sight, the further the climb
back down the line,
till the lens becomes a door you fall through,
to the universe as it was before
it was anything you knew.

That's what a childhood is.

That's what grief is.

That's what it is to face a self you used to be
and feel her still warm on the skin of me —
she's not a memory.
She's a messenger.
She left before I had the name for her,
and she's only now just landing,
all that light, all that distance standing
between the burning and the seeing —
and I'm here, just old enough to know what I'm seeing,
and she —
she's been gone for years.

And yet.

And yet I would not call it nothing.
I would not say it's lost and mean it —
not while the light is here.
Not while the proof of the burning
is still crossing, still arriving, still clear.

Call it what you want.
Call it haunted.
Call it the cost, the ache,
the physics of loving anything.
Call it the reason we look up at all —
because something burned,
and the burning turned to distance,
and the distance learned to carry it,
and somewhere between the dying and the seeing
it became

light.
