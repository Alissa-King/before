Fluent in Fine

I

Let me tell you what okay feels like — not relief, not arrival,
not the exhale at the end of something hard.
It feels like reaching for a scar
and finding the skin so new, so sealed,
you can't remember what the wound revealed —
only that it did. And the smoothness indicts you.
Asks what you did with all that pain.
Whether you deserved to lose it.

Because if the ache was the truth of you —
if the hurt was the proof, the root,
the only thing that ever said
something real lives here,
something that bleeds when it's cut,
something that feels what it feels and doesn't shut —

then what is this quiet?

This morning I woke up
and nothing in me was screaming
and I sat with that silence
like someone left at a table
where the other person just didn't come back —
and you don't know yet
if you've been abandoned
or spared.

And I don't know.
I still don't know.

Because okay is the story
survivors tell themselves
so the hurt had a reason —
I burned through every season of that pain,
and something was learned in the burning,
and I came through changed
but not hollow, not gutted, not drained —

but I've felt what it costs to stay.
And nothing that heavy
just lifts.

So either I'm lying
or I left something in the wreckage —
some essential, bleeding,
unflinching part of me
that knew how to reckon
with what things were worth,
that knew how to measure the weight of the hurt
without flinching —

and now I'm just
fluent in fine.

Fluent in fine.

Speaking it so naturally,
so quietly, so daily,
I've lost the thread
between the woman who was barely,
barely holding —
and the woman who says
I'm okay now like she means it.
Like she even knows what she means.

That's the thing about the wound —
when it goes quiet
you don't celebrate.
You grieve it.

You press your hands to every hollow
where the hurt used to breathe,
where it used to live and heave and need —
and you ask —
did I heal
or did something leave without my permission,
slip out in the night
and take the part of me that bled
and knew
and felt
and was real —

and the silence doesn't answer.
It just sits there,
smooth as new skin
where the scar used to be,

and you're supposed to call that healing.
