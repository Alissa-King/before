The Back of Her Head

V

0:00

The house has no locks
and the doors never close,
and every room is burning with light,
and I suppose
that's the cruelty of it —
not the dark,
but the glow
of a room she just left,
that I'm just too slow
to catch her in.

I am always too slow.

And the one thing
I ever seem to know
of any of them
is the back of her head
going through the far door.

She's six in the kitchen.
I'd know that hair anywhere —
knew it before I knew the word for mine.
I cross to reach her
and the floor's not fair,
it stretches, it lengthens,
it doubles the line between us,
and she's at the threshold,
a shoulder, a heel,
the sleeve of a dress
I put in the ground a long time ago —

and then the doorway holds nothing at all
but the feel of the light she left it in.

And I know that glow.

I have stood in that warmth.
It is always still warm.
The cup on the table,
still ringed with the heat of her hands.
The chair still holding her form.
The fog of her breath on the glass,
the not-yet-cold seat —

she was here.

She is always just barely just-here,
and the just is the whole of the ache,
the just is the blade,
the hair's-width of nothing
I can't get clear,
the half-second late
I am always,
always
made.

I climb to the room
where the twenty-three-year-old stays.
I knew her so well
I could wear her like a coat,
and her certainty still hangs in the air
like the haze of a perfume
that outlasts the throat that it rose from —

and I call the name that we share,
and the curtain lifts,
and my heart climbs up to meet —

and it's only the window,
gone wide on the empty air,
and her, at the edge of the frame,
and her,
in retreat.

I never once see their faces.
That's the deal.

I have wandered this whole loud house of my life
learning the backs of my own heads —
the way they lean into a door,
the slope of a shoulder I'd know in any light,
the nape, the turning that never turns,
each of them leaving the second I arrive,
each of them gone
down a hall where the next room burns —

and here is the thing
I have never said out loud,
the thing at the floor of the ache,
under the crowd,
under the chasing,
under the years I have spent
calling their names down corridors:

I am glad.

I am glad they never turn.

Because the day the six-year-old
stops in the door
and turns,
and sees me —
sees the whole of what I bore her toward,
the rooms I led that bright thing into,
the use I made
of all that she once knew —

I will have to stand
in the light I couldn't outrun
and answer for it.

Every one.
Everyone.

The certain one will turn
and ask me where
I put her certainty,
and I will have to open
my empty hands
and show her

what I was given.
What I have left.
How little
of the one
became the other.

So I keep arriving late.
I have learned to come a half-step slow.
I have made the missing
into a kind of grace,
because a hallway full of the backs of heads
is a house I can still live in.

But a single face —
one of them,
turning,
knowing —

and the whole thing breaks.

I am never alone in here.
I am never alone.

And I have wanted,
in the loud and the burning,
to keep it just so —
to chase,
and to almost,
and to let the almost go,
to trail her shadow through the door
and let her stay
one room ahead of the reckoning,
one doorway clear
of the turn,
of the stop,
of the moment she finally sees
exactly who was always,
always
just behind her.

Let her run.

Let the light stay warm.

Let not one of them —

not the child,

not the certain one,

not a single soul
I've been
and shed
and grieved —

ever

turn

around.
