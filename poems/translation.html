III

Everyone who loves me

loves a version.
Not me — a me made sayable,

rendered down to something they could hold,

edited for the parts they had the eyes to see,

the parts that fit the mold

of who they needed me to be.

And I helped.

I helped.

I handed them the gentler draft —
the me with the sharp edges filed,
the grief left vague,
the hunger torn clean off the page,
because some rooms can't hold the whole of you
and you learn to enter sideways,
learn to leave the unspeakable parts
outside the door
like shoes.

So they love me.

They do.

But they love the me that made it through —
that survived being rendered
into something small enough to carry,
something a person could hold
without having to learn
a whole new tongue
to hold it.

And I wonder, in the dark,
in the after,
when the talking's done
and they're breathing the slow,
untroubled sleep
of someone sure they know me —

I wonder if the truest part of me
is just the part that wouldn't go.

The residue. The remainder.
The word that died mid-sentence
on the bridge
from what I am
to what I let you see —
because something always dies on that bridge,
and I think it's the realest thing in me.

There's always a word in the original
with no echo in the new,
a word that means
the ache of being almost reached —
and it drowns mid-crossing,
halfway between
the two of us,
each one straining,
each one reaching,
each one landing

just

beside.

And maybe that's the closest anyone gets.

Maybe to be known
is to be lovingly misread
by someone who'd learn you to the bone
if the learning could be done —
if we weren't each of us
locked behind the glass
of our own seeing —
palms pressed white,
mouthing the truth we mean,
watching it fog the pane
before it crosses
the small, unbreakable
distance
between.

I used to think the loneliness
was going unseen.
Now I think it's meaner than that.

It's being seen
almost.

Held in a likeness of yourself
that's close —
close enough to love,
close enough to grieve against,
but never close enough
to set the whole of you down
and say

here.

this.

this is the thing I actually am —

and watch them

nod

and mean it.

We're all just
beloved approximations
in the minds of the ones who hold us.

And maybe — please —
maybe being loved beside the truth
is still being loved.
Maybe the reaching counts.
Maybe two people
straining to read each other
in a language neither speaks clean
is the most that love can be —

and maybe I can learn
to call that

enough.

Or maybe I'll go my whole life
almost-known,
beloved and beside,
held warm and certain
in the wrong arms
of the right people —

and never learn
if this ache I carry
is love

failing —

or just

its

shape.
